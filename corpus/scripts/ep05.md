# THE TIDES OF VASSEN
## Episode 5 — "The Cracked Stone"

POV: Mira Vance.

---

# SCENE 1 — EXT. UPPER CITY — A LAMPLIGHTER'S POLE — DAWN

A lamplighter, 50s, on his rounds. He reaches up with the long brass cap and snuffs a single iron lantern at the corner of the Council Wall. The wick goes out under the cap. He moves on.

The Council Wall, behind him, is fifty feet of cut basalt and iron grilles. Above it, a single black banner — Councilor Thane's house mark — moves once in the wind.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 2 — INT. PRECINCT INFIRMARY — MORNING

Mira on the cot. The bandage on her temple is fresh. Her left eye is swollen but open.

Hale at the bedside. Verity beside him, her notebook in her hand.

MIRA
Inspector.

HALE
Mira.

MIRA
They took the Compass.

HALE
I know.

MIRA
I should've—

HALE
You should not have. You did what you could. We have the Ledger.

A beat.

MIRA
The roof-route.

HALE
You'll show us.

MIRA
I'll lead.

HALE
You won't. You'll show. I'll lead.

A small smile. The first since Joss.

MIRA
(quiet)
Yes, Inspector.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 3 — INT. HALE'S OFFICE — MIDDAY

The Black Ledger open on the desk. Verity at the door. Tobin Brask just leaving with a sealed reply for the Dredgemaster.

Verity writes on a slip and hands it to Hale.

NOTE (insert)
*The fourth stanza has not been carved. He has not yet had a body for it. If we move tonight, he will never have one.*

He nods. He folds the note. He locks it in the drawer.

He looks at the Ledger. He has, in the last twelve hours, read enough of it to know that Thane has paid Brenn since 1894, the Black Watch since 1881, three other Councilors since 1879, and the Salt Exchange's senior auditor since 1898 — the year Eline died.

He has not yet shown the Ledger to Brenn. He will not.

He goes to the door of the small adjoining cell where the wounded Watchman from Episode 3 was held. The cell is empty.

The duty officer at the desk says only, *Released by privilege. Council ring. Eight bells.*

Hale does not change his face. He notes the time. He goes back to his desk.

He writes, on a slip, *He knows we have the man's name. He will move tonight.* He folds it. He gives it to Verity.

She nods.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 4 — EXT. UPPER CITY — A ROOFTOP — EVENING

Mira leads them. She has a coil of climber's line at her hip. Her temple is bandaged under her cap. Her shoulder is stiff but not weak.

Hale and Verity follow. Hale has the Black Ledger inside his coat lining; Verity has the warrant inside hers.

They cross three rooftops. They drop to a parapet. They cross a courtyard at gutter-height. Mira does this the way a fish does a current.

They come to the side wall of Thane's house. A small iron grille at second-floor height, painted to match the stone, gives onto a service stair.

MIRA
Here.

HALE
You know this house.

MIRA
I've been on its roof since I was twelve. Never inside. Today's a first.

She lifts the grille. It is unlocked from the inside.

VERITY (writing in haste, holding it up)
*Why unlocked.*

HALE
Servants' door. The Watch coming and going.

MIRA
Or he wants us in.

HALE
He doesn't know we're here.

MIRA
We hope.

She drops through the grille. Verity follows. Hale last. He pulls the grille closed behind him.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 5 — INT. THANE'S HOUSE — SERVICE STAIR — EVENING

A narrow stair, lamplit at the top. They climb. They emerge into a back hall. A long carpet, dark.

A door to the left, ajar. A cold draft from inside.

Verity looks at Hale. He nods.

She pushes the door open.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 6 — INT. THANE'S HOUSE — COLD-ROOM — CONTINUOUS

A small windowless room, walled in tile. Ice on the floor in shallow trays. A low cot. A still figure on the cot.

A woman, mid-fifties, gaunt, hands folded across her chest. She has been dead three weeks. The cold has held her.

Verity stops in the doorway.

She does not speak. She has not spoken aloud in two days. She does not write either. She walks into the room. She kneels by the cot. She takes the woman's hand in both of hers.

Hale stands in the doorway. Mira behind him.

The woman is Eilan Crane. Verity's mother. The forged letters were forged from a dead hand for three weeks.

Verity rises. Her face is not yet a face. She takes her notebook from her coat. She writes, slowly, a single line, and hands it to Hale.

NOTEBOOK PAGE (insert, Verity's hand)
*He is in the study.*

CUT TO:

# SCENE 7 — INT. THANE'S STUDY — EVENING

A high-ceilinged room. A desk of black walnut. A single lamp. The framed parchment of the 1881 Lantern Edict on the wall.

THELONIUS THANE, in shirtsleeves at his desk, looks up as they enter. He is not, visibly, surprised. He has been, in fact, expecting them since morning.

THANE
Inspector. Sergeant. Miss Vance.

He inclines his head.

THANE
Will you sit.

HALE
No.

THANE
As you like.

Verity steps forward. She lifts the notebook. She writes one word and shows it.

NOTEBOOK PAGE (insert, Verity's hand)
*Why.*

Thane reads. He sets down his pen.

THANE
Sergeant. The simple answer is your mother was a useful price for a long-running purchase. The complicated answer requires a chair. As you do not wish to sit, I will offer the simple.

He stands. He walks to a locked cabinet against the wall. He takes a small brass key from his waistcoat pocket. He unlocks the cabinet.

Inside, on a velvet cushion, a glass jar. In the jar, in a clear brine, a woman's left hand. The index finger has a small dark stain at the cuticle — old ink, salt-fixed.

He lifts the jar out and sets it on the desk.

THANE
Inspector. You have asked, in your own private way, every year since 1898, what happened to your wife's left hand.

Hale's face does a small thing. The garnet ring at his throat is suddenly cold against his collarbone.

THANE
She was in the Salt Exchange's sealed records the day she died. She copied an entry that named my father. She had ink on this finger when I asked her to walk with me. I removed the hand because the ink would have been a witness.

A long beat. Hale does not draw. He does not, in this room, with this man, draw yet.

HALE
You drowned her.

THANE
In the shallows. She did not fight. She had, by then, understood.

HALE
You took her ring.

THANE
I did.

HALE
You took mine.

THANE
A souvenir. Forgive me.

He opens the top drawer of his desk. He removes a small velvet pouch. He places it on the desk.

THANE
Yours.

Hale does not lift it. He looks at it. He nods once. He does not lift it yet.

THANE
The verses came to me two ways. She gave me a copy at the Exchange — I asked, and she gave. The other came off her body. The two have lived in this house together for five years.

HALE
You carved them.

THANE
I did.

HALE
On Marsh.

THANE
On Marsh.

HALE
On Trent. On Vance.

THANE
Trent for unrelated reasons that the verse made convenient. Marsh and Vance for the verse itself.

HALE
Why the verse.

THANE
A symmetry. She had asked, in 1898, how a city forgets. I had told her it forgets by writing the truth onto things it then drowns. She wrote a poem about it that night. I am, twenty years on, finishing the poem on the city's behalf.

A pause. Verity, behind Hale, has been writing. She lifts the notebook.

NOTEBOOK PAGE (insert, Verity's hand)
*You used me to draw her shade.*

THANE
I did.

NOTEBOOK PAGE (insert, Verity's hand)
*Why through her shade. Why not your own copy.*

THANE
Because the verse I have is finite, Sergeant. Twenty-one stanzas. I was fond of the thought of more. Your gift was the only way to ask her for them. Your shade-work draws from the dead what they themselves remember; she remembers her verses better than my paper does.

NOTEBOOK PAGE (insert, Verity's hand)
*That is not a rule of any working I know.*

THANE
No. It isn't. Your gift is older than the rules anyone in this room learned. I had an account of it from your grandmother's physician.

A long beat.

THANE
You should know, Sergeant, that the cost of those workings to your voice is not the ordinary cost. You have aged twenty years against your gift in two weeks. I am sorry. I had not predicted how sharp.

He lifts a small black tourmaline stone from the desk. It is uncracked. It is black; it is whole; it is charged.

THANE
Inspector. This is your wife's last memory. Drawn by the Sergeant from her shade three nights ago, on the night you saw the Sergeant at the Bay. The stone holds the moment she walked into the water. It is uncracked. It is unread.

He sets the stone on the desk beside the velvet pouch.

THANE
Read it, Inspector. You should know.

A long, long beat.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 8 — INT. THANE'S STUDY — CONTINUOUS

Hale draws his service revolver. He does not aim it at Thane. He lays it, instead, across his own forearm, the muzzle pointed at the floor.

HALE
The Compass.

THANE
In the Council vault. As you know.

HALE
We will go there now. The four of us. You will open the vault. You will hand me the Compass. You will then walk out of this house under arrest.

THANE
That is not, I think, what's going to happen.

HALE
Walk.

THANE
Inspector. There is a Watch detail in the courtyard. There has been one since you came through the grille. The grille was unlocked because I unlocked it.

HALE
And.

THANE
And, with me on my feet and walking, the Watch will not enter. With me on my back, they will. With me dead, they will burn this house to the foundations and there will be no Ledger and no hand and no stone for any record to find.

A beat.

HALE
We're walking, Councilor.

THANE
On my feet. As you say.

He picks up the ebony cane he does not need. He gestures, very politely, to the door.

HALE
Sergeant. The hand, the stone, the pouch. Take them.

Verity gathers the glass jar, the tourmaline, the velvet pouch. She tucks the jar into her oilcloth coat.

HALE
Mira.

MIRA
Inspector.

HALE
Lead.

She leads.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 9 — EXT. THANE'S HOUSE — COURTYARD — NIGHT

A light rain. Two Black Watch in oilcloth-black at the gate. They lower their iron-tipped batons when they see Thane upright.

Thane gives a small, almost imperceptible head-shake. The Watch step aside.

The four of them — Hale, Verity, Mira, Thane — pass out of the house and into a waiting Council carriage Thane has, with characteristic foresight, kept harnessed since dusk.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 10 — INT. COUNCIL CHAMBER — LOWER VAULT — NIGHT

A vast, low-ceilinged chamber. The roof is iron — sheets bolted to oak beams. A single bronze door at the far end. A row of lamps in iron sconces along the wall. The Compass, in its replacement salt-cedar box, sits on a stone plinth in the center.

The Dredgemaster, returned to the city as he promised, stands beside the plinth. His iron three-toothed key is back on its cord around his neck. He has unlocked the vault for them. He is bleeding lightly through his bandaged forearm.

Thane sees the Dredgemaster. The two old men look at each other. Neither speaks.

DREDGEMASTER (quietly)
Olen Brask, Inspector. Twenty-two years late, but here.

HALE
Thank you, Olen.

DREDGEMASTER
The vault was sealed with my key, by their request, in 1881. I never thought I'd open it again. The other key is on his ring.

He gestures at Thane with his cane.

THANE
The other key.

He extends his right hand. The ring, the compass-rose, gold and black. He twists the front face of the ring; the back of it folds out into a small hooked pick. A second key.

THANE
A small piece of jewelry, Dredgemaster.

DREDGEMASTER
A bigger one than mine, by the look.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 11 — INT. COUNCIL CHAMBER — LOWER VAULT — CONTINUOUS

Hale steps to the plinth. He lifts the Compass from its salt-cedar box. The needle, in his palm, swings. Under the iron roof it should not move. It does not move. The room is dead.

He carries it to the bronze door. He lays it on the threshold — half inside the iron-roofed vault, half on the stone outside, where moonlight falls from a slit window.

The needle, half-out, swings. It points at Thane's chest.

HALE
We have not been three minutes in this room and already, with one foot of needle in moonlight, you are the lie.

THANE
A useful instrument. I have always disliked it.

HALE
Olen. Your hammer.

The Dredgemaster lifts his iron-tipped cane.

THANE
Inspector. One thing.

HALE
What.

THANE
The stone. You haven't read it.

HALE
I haven't.

THANE
You should. It is the only piece of her you will ever have again. Once you destroy the Compass, the rest of this room will follow, and the stone with it.

HALE
The stone won't.

THANE
Won't?

HALE
The stone won't follow.

A beat.

THANE
Read it, Inspector.

HALE
No.

THANE
She wanted you to know.

A very long beat.

HALE
She wanted me to know what she wanted me to know in life. She is owed her death by herself.

He nods to the Dredgemaster.

The Dredgemaster lifts the iron-tipped cane. He brings it down, hard, on the Compass.

The brass casing splinters. The needle, an inch of fine-forged iron, snaps clean across. The two halves spin once on the stone and stop.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 12 — INT. COUNCIL CHAMBER — LOWER VAULT — CONTINUOUS

Thane, very fast for a man of sixty-one, lunges. Not at Hale. At the broken Compass — at the unmoving halves on the stone. He has, in his hand, suddenly, a small knife from inside the cane.

He says one word as he goes—

THANE
*Half.*

Hale fires.

The first shot goes wide — into the iron-tipped cane, which cracks and falls.

Hale fires again.

The second shot takes Thane in the chest, just below the sternum. He folds. He goes to one knee. The knife slips from his hand.

He looks up at Hale. He is not, even now, surprised. He is, perhaps, mildly disappointed that he has miscalculated.

THANE
(whispered)
*Read it. You should know.*

His head bows. He is dead.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 13 — INT. COUNCIL CHAMBER — LOWER VAULT — CONTINUOUS

A long silence. The vault smells of cordite and salt.

Verity walks to Thane. She kneels. She closes his eyes. Then she stands, very steadily, and walks back to Hale.

She lifts her notebook. She writes nothing. She does not need to.

Mira, by the door, wipes her cheek with the back of her hand. The first time tonight.

The Dredgemaster gathers the broken halves of the Compass and folds them into a square of canvas from his coat pocket.

DREDGEMASTER
For the Bay.

HALE
For the Bay.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 14 — EXT. SOUTHERN POINT — VERY LATE NIGHT

The four of them — Hale, Verity, Mira, the Dredgemaster — stand at the seawall. The tide is at its low. The exposed shingle is wet. The moon is past full and declining.

The Dredgemaster steps to the wall. He unfolds the canvas square. He throws the broken halves of the Compass into the shallows below.

The pieces hit the wet shingle and lie there, half-buried, the iron needle catching no light. The next tide will take them out.

He stands a long time at the wall.

DREDGEMASTER
(to the Bay, at low tide)
Keep her, then. She was never ours.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 15 — EXT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STUDY WINDOW — JUST BEFORE DAWN

Hale at the window. The uncracked memory-stone is on the deal table. The velvet pouch with his gold ring is beside it. The glass jar with Eline's left hand has been placed, by Hale, in a small cedar chest he has not opened in five years; he will bury the hand at sea at the next bay-burial.

He lifts the gold ring from the velvet. He turns it once. He sets it back. He closes the velvet.

He does not put the ring on his finger.

He picks up a small tin box from the mantel. He opens it. Inside is a yellowed letter from Eline, dated 1898. He has not read it in five years. He does not read it tonight either.

He places the gold ring inside the tin box. He closes the box. He sets the box back on the mantel.

The garnet ring on the iron chain remains at his throat.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 16 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — KITCHEN — DAWN

Verity at the deal table. She has a cup of salt-water tea. Her notebook is closed.

Hale sits across from her.

HALE
The stone.

She nods.

HALE
I will not read it.

She nods.

HALE
I want to ask why you tried to speak last night, on the beach, when you could not.

A long beat. She looks at him. She lifts her hand. She presses her gloved fingers to her own throat. She closes her eyes.

She opens her eyes. She tries.

VERITY
(with great effort, voice hoarse and broken, but a voice)
*She wanted you to know.*

It is the only sentence she will speak in the rest of the season.

He does not weep. He nods, once.

He puts his hand over hers.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 17 — EXT. VASSEN BAY — A SEA-BURIAL — MORNING

A small boat. The Dredgemaster at the tiller. Mira at the bow. Joss Vance, weighted in a shroud, on the deck.

Mira speaks. Her voice is low, accented, tired.

MIRA
*Joss Pell Vance. Born 14 Drimmar 1879. Died 17 Drimmar 1903. Never lied to me about a thing that mattered.*

She slides the shroud over the rail. The boat's wake closes.

The Dredgemaster lifts a smaller shroud — a glass jar, weighted in cloth. He holds it out to Hale, who has come aboard at the harbor bell. Hale takes the jar. He lowers it himself, one hand on the rail.

It sinks.

HALE
(to the Bay)
Eline Adair Hale. 1864 to 1898. Her name in the air.

He lets the rail go. The boat rocks once.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 18 — INT. HALE'S OFFICE — LATE AFTERNOON

Hale at his desk. The drawer is open. The verses are no longer in the oilcloth wrapper; they are loose on the blotter, twenty-one stanzas, in his hand. He gathers them. He folds them. He puts them in the cedar chest at home, the one with Eline's hand.

The uncracked memory-stone is on the desk in front of him.

He looks at it a long time.

He does not read it.

He rises. He takes a small velvet pouch from his coat. He lowers the stone into the pouch. He ties the pouch shut. He places the pouch in the locked drawer.

He locks the drawer.

He writes, on a clean sheet of paper—

HALE'S WRITING (insert)
*Cadet of the Constabulary of Vassen.*
*Letter of consideration for Miss Mira Pell Vance, age nineteen, of the Lower Wards.*
*Submitted by Inspector Casimir A. Hale, badge forty-one.*

He signs it.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 19 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT WALL — NIGHT

A different Watchman, on a different night. He chalks his patrol-board: *18 Drimmar 1903. All quiet.*

Behind him, in the moonlit silver of the abandoned streets, the water laps an inch higher than the night before.

He hangs the chalk.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 20 — EXT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — VERY LATE — FINAL SCENE

The lantern over the cottage door has been out since the night of Episode 1.

Hale steps onto the porch with a fresh wick, a small tin of lamp-oil, and a match.

He trims the wick. He fills the lantern. He strikes the match. He lights it.

The flame catches.

The lantern burns.

He stands a long moment with his hand on the door, looking at the light.

Verity, inside, watches him through the small window. She does not write. She does not need to.

The tide, far below, comes in an inch farther than the day before.

It always does.

FADE TO BLACK.
