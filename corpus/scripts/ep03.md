# THE TIDES OF VASSEN
## Episode 3 — "The Black Ledger"

POV: Sergeant Verity Crane.

---

# SCENE 1 — EXT. SOUTHERN BEACH — BAY EDGE — PRE-DAWN

A single iron lantern, planted in the sand at the high-water mark by a fisher hours ago. The wick has burned to its base. It guts and goes.

The tide comes in an inch. The lantern, knocked by the wave, falls onto its side. Its empty chimney rolls.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 2 — INT. CRANE APARTMENT — PRE-DAWN

Verity sits on the edge of her bed, fully dressed. Her oilcloth notebook is open on her knee. The forged letter is in her hand, opened to its second sheet, which arrived under her door this morning.

THE SECOND LETTER (insert)
*Sergeant — your mother has had a very bad night. The wasting is on her chest now. We do not yet despair. The Councilor's patronage has been increased again. — Dr. Pell.*

She folds the letter. She slides it under the mattress with the first.

She reads, in her notebook, the lines she wrote at the Bay's edge two nights ago. Five lines. She reads them aloud, very softly, in her own hoarsening voice—

VERITY
*The harbor closed its iron eye*—

She stops. She presses her hand to her throat. Her voice, this morning, is not quite hers.

She does not say the rest of the stanza aloud.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 3 — INT. HARBOR PRECINCT — HALE'S OFFICE — MORNING

Hale at his desk. The locked drawer is open. The verses are spread across the blotter, all twenty-one stanzas in his hand. He is checking page numbers.

VERITY enters.

VERITY
You wanted me.

HALE
Sit.

She sits. She is steady. He watches her.

HALE
Your voice.

VERITY
A cold. Off the harbor. It'll pass.

HALE
The almanac says the cold's gone past the harbor. We're under a southern wind.

A beat. She does not answer. He does not push. Yet.

HALE
I want eyes on the Eastern District wall tonight.

VERITY
The Watch will see us.

HALE
Two of us, no lantern, by the cooper's row. Mira can put us on a roof.

VERITY
You're not thirty anymore, Inspector.

HALE
And you're hoarse. We'll mind each other.

A small laugh from her. She does not mean for it to sound forced. It sounds forced.

She rises. She nods. She goes.

He watches the door close. He notes, in pencil, in the margin of stanza three: *unread.*

CUT TO:

# SCENE 4 — EXT. SALT EXCHANGE STEPS — MIDDAY

Mira on the wall above the steps. She is on a high coping she has used for two years; no one looks up. Below her, on the broad steps, Black Watch couriers come and go.

A courier — heavyset, oilcloth-black — hurries past with a leather satchel. Something falls from the satchel as he turns the corner. Small, brass. It catches the noon light.

He does not notice. He keeps going.

Mira drops from the coping in three movements — wall, awning, step — and has the small brass thing in her palm before another hand can reach it.

It is the Vassen Compass.

The needle, in her hand, swings and points at her own throat.

She frowns. She does not know what it is. She closes her fist around it and walks, very ordinary, down the steps and into the crowd.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 5 — INT. MIRA'S ROOM — LOWER WARDS — AFTERNOON

A narrow room above a fish-cellar. A single window. A pallet. A small shelf with the clay charm of the Drowned Mother on it.

Mira lays the Compass on the floor. She sits cross-legged. She watches the needle.

The needle moves. It points, steadily, at her own throat. She turns; it follows.

She has heard, since she was small, of the Compass that points at lies within seven paces. She has not, until now, believed it was real.

She is alone in the room. She is the only living thing in seven paces.

She thinks for a long time about which lie of hers, alive in her own throat, the needle is naming.

She slides the Compass under a loose floorboard and shuts the room.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 6 — INT. EASTERN DISTRICT — A FLOODED BASEMENT — EARLY EVENING

Mira in the basement. She has crossed the wall by a gap she has known about since she was twelve — a place where the basalt has been undermined by tide-rot, an opening just wide enough for a small body. She has been here three times in her life. Never twice in the same year.

She has come because Joss has not been home in two nights.

The basement is half-flooded. Water to the knee. A rotted staircase. A stone floor under the water.

She finds him. Face up. Two inches of black water lapping his collar. His shirt cut up the centre, folded back. Six lines carved into his sternum, fresh. Blood still beading at the edges of the deepest cut.

She does not scream. She is a Lower Wards girl. She kneels in the water. She lays her hand on his face.

She speaks, low, in their childhood half-language.

MIRA
Joss. Joss. You stupid bastard. Joss.

She closes his eyes.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 7 — INT. EASTERN DISTRICT BASEMENT — MOMENTS LATER

She lifts her head. She has work. She is good at work in bad rooms.

She climbs the rotted staircase to the half-room above. A high shelf. A tin bread-box. She has noticed it on the way in but has not had a hand free.

She stretches up. She pulls the bread-box down. She opens it.

A heavy leather book. Stamped on the cover: a sigil she does not, at this moment, recognise. A ringed compass-rose. Black wax inside the gold tooling.

She tucks the book under her oilcloth jerkin. She sets the bread-box back on the shelf as she found it.

She climbs down. She passes Joss without stopping. She does not look back.

The fish-knife in her boot is the only thing between her and another body, but the killer is gone. She knows that. The killer is always gone.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 8 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT WALL — A GAP IN THE BASALT — NIGHT

Mira squeezes through. She is bleeding from one shoulder where she scraped on the stone. She does not stop to bind it.

Far across the wet flagstones, on the harbor side, she sees, briefly, two figures on the cooper's row roof. She knows one of them by silhouette: Inspector Hale.

She does not call out. He has told her to stay off the wall this week. She is off the wall as fast as she can go.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 9 — EXT. COOPER'S ROW — ROOFTOP — NIGHT

Hale and Verity, low on the slate. Verity has a small spy-glass; she has the better eyes. Hale has the almanac in his coat.

VERITY
(low)
There. East corner. A patrol of three.

HALE
And.

VERITY
Past the patrol. A figure at the wall. Single. Small.

HALE
The wall outside or in.

VERITY
In. Coming out through the gap below the basalt notch.

HALE
Mira.

VERITY
Hale—

HALE
She's clear of them. Don't follow.

A beat. Verity, who has the better eyes, sees Mira crouch a long second by the gap, then bolt for the alley.

A patrol passes a hundred yards along the wall, never turning their heads. Mira is already gone.

VERITY
(quiet)
She came out of there with something.

HALE
She always does.

VERITY
Bleeding.

HALE
She always is.

A beat. Then, almost in spite of himself—

HALE
We'll find her in the morning.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 10 — INT. MIRA'S ROOM — VERY LATE

Mira on the pallet. The Black Ledger under the loose board with the Compass. Her shoulder bandaged, badly, with a strip torn from her own shirt.

She has Joss's folded sketch in her hand. She has not let it go.

She does not weep yet. The weeping will come tomorrow, on the walk to the bay-burial that is not yet allowed because the body is still in the District. It will come at the third bell of the morning, when she can no longer keep it in.

A lantern in the alley below her window guts and recovers.

She watches it.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 11 — EXT. SOUTHERN POINT — BAY EDGE — SAME NIGHT

Verity again. Hooded. The notebook. The pencil.

She has come because the second letter under her door said, *The corporal's shade also speaks. Walk again. Same place. Same hour. Same payment.*

She kneels at the waterline. She lays her right palm on the wet sand. She lifts her left wrist.

She speaks, low, in Tide-tongue.

VERITY (in Tide-tongue, subtitled)
Caelis Trent. Drowned not in the salt but in the spring. Carved, but not by me. Speak.

A long beat. The water listens.

A VOICE comes up from the Bay. The same warm, careful voice as before. The same woman in her early thirties.

VOICE FROM THE BAY (Eline Hale's voice, V.O.)
*Three lanterns at the inner wall / and three the salt put out. / The boy who ran the eastern lane / heard what the Watch was about.*

Four lines. Verity writes. A fifth—

VOICE FROM THE BAY (cont'd)
*And what the Watch was about, my love, / was the sluice that drowned the town.*

She finishes. She reads. *My love.* The shade has called her *my love.*

She is no one's love. Not in this voice. Not from this water.

Her hand shakes. She tears the page out. She folds it.

She stands. She presses her palm to her throat. The hoarseness in her voice is worse tonight, and she knows now, without yet allowing herself to know, that the Tide-mark is on her two decades early.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 12 — EXT. SOUTHERN POINT — A LITTLE INLAND — CONTINUOUS

She walks back. She slides the page under the third stone of the seawall steps. She does not look back at the Bay.

A hundred yards behind her, on the basalt above the cove, Mira Vance kneels low. Mira has come out to the southern beach because she could not be in her own room any longer. She has seen Verity walking. She has seen Verity speaking to nothing.

She does not understand what she has seen. She does not call out.

She watches Verity disappear up the path, and then she watches the page under the stone disappear, twenty minutes later, into a gloved hand attached to a dark coat she does not know how to read.

She tells no one.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 13 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STUDY — NIGHT

Hale at his desk. The folded copy of the verses spread before him. He is checking, line by line, what stanza is which.

The first stanza: *the harbor closed its iron eye*. The second: *the clerk who bled at the Eastern wall*. The third—

He stops at the third. He reads it slowly.

VERSE INSERT
*Three lanterns at the inner wall*
*and three the salt put out.*
*The boy who ran the eastern lane*
*heard what the Watch was about.*
*And what the Watch was about, my love,*
*was the sluice that drowned the town.*

Six lines, not five. Eline wrote the third stanza in six. He had forgotten that. He had not checked.

He checks the page numbers in his copy. Stanza 1, page one. Stanza 2, page one. Stanza 3, page two. They run on.

He folds the verses back into the oilcloth wrapper. He locks the drawer.

Then, very slowly, he draws his service revolver, opens the cylinder, checks the load, snaps it shut, and sets it on the desk.

He does not put it back under his coat.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 14 — INT. HARBOR PRECINCT — HALE'S OFFICE — DAWN

A constable runs in. Young. Out of breath.

CONSTABLE
Inspector. Eastern District. A body. The salt-runner girl found him.

HALE
Mira Vance.

CONSTABLE
She came to the precinct an hour ago. Wouldn't sit. Wouldn't talk. Wrote it on a slip and left.

HALE
A boy.

CONSTABLE
Her brother.

A beat. Hale closes his eyes for the length of one breath.

HALE
Sergeant Crane.

CONSTABLE
I called for her.

HALE
Tell her to meet me at the cottage. Not the precinct. The cottage.

CONSTABLE
Sir.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 15 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT — JOSS VANCE BASEMENT — MORNING

Hale, alone, has come the long way — by Council warrant, hastily issued. The Watchman at the gate is a different one this morning, surly, unhappy.

He goes down the rotted stairs. The body is where Mira found him. The water has risen another inch overnight; Joss's left ear is just under the surface.

Hale crouches. The verse on the chest. Stanza three, six lines. The third line reads, *the boy who ran the eastern lane*. Hale closes his eyes.

He notices the tin bread-box on the high shelf. He notices it the way one notices a thing that has been moved. The dust on the shelf has a clean square where the box used to sit dead-centre, and the box now sits half an inch off-square.

He does not climb up to look inside. He files it.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 16 — INT. EASTERN DISTRICT BASEMENT — MOMENTS LATER

A scrape on the stairs. Hale draws the revolver. He does not yet aim.

A figure descends. Oilcloth-black. Watch coat. The Watchman from the gate, but no — broader. Not the gate Watchman.

The man sees Hale and freezes. He has a club in his hand.

Hale does not say *halt.* He does not say *drop it.* He says—

HALE
Tell me.

The Watchman's hand twitches at his belt — an iron knife. Hale fires once. The shot takes the man in the right wrist. The knife drops into the water with a small sound. The Watchman drops to his knees in the water and grips his ruined hand.

Hale does not fire again.

HALE
Who sent you.

WATCHMAN
(through teeth)
Get off me.

HALE
Who.

WATCHMAN
Get—

HALE
A name.

WATCHMAN
(spitting)
Get a warrant.

A long beat. Hale lowers the revolver but does not holster it. He grips the man's collar with his free hand and hauls him up.

HALE
Iron cuffs.

He clicks an iron cuff onto the man's good wrist. The man hisses through his teeth from the pain in his ruined wrist as the cuff settles.

Hale leads him up the stairs.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 17 — EXT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STEPS — LATE AFTERNOON

Verity sits on the cottage step. She has been there an hour. The notebook is in her lap. She has written nothing.

Hale comes up the path, soaked from the District, a bandage on his coat sleeve where Joss's water has stained it.

She stands.

VERITY
Inspector.

HALE
Sergeant.

VERITY
Mira.

HALE
She's at the precinct. They have her in the warm room. She won't speak.

VERITY
She found him.

HALE
Yes.

A beat.

VERITY
(very quiet)
Inspector. The verses.

HALE
Yes.

VERITY
There were two copies.

HALE
Yes.

VERITY
The other one is still somewhere.

HALE
Yes.

VERITY
And the killer has it.

HALE
The killer has had it five years.

A long beat. Verity puts her hand on her throat without thinking. She does not say what she might say.

She turns. She walks down the path.

He watches her go. He notices, again, the hoarseness. He notices, this time, that it is two days worse and that there is no cold on the harbor.

He files it.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 18 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — KITCHEN — NIGHT

Hale alone. He has a sheet of paper and a pencil. He has begun, without meaning to, a list.

THE LIST (insert, in his hand)
*Marsh — Eastern District — stanza I — cracked stone in mouth*
*Trent — cistern — stanza II — no stone*
*Vance — Eastern District basement — stanza III — no stone (yet)*
*Verses — two copies — mine, and one*
*The other copy — Council, by Salt Exchange route?*
*Compass — stolen — Watch — for whom?*

Beneath the list, after a long pause, he writes—

*Verity. Hoarse two days. Was at the Bay last night.*

He looks at that line a long time.

He folds the list and slides it inside the locked drawer with the verses.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 19 — INT. CRANE APARTMENT — VERY LATE

Verity in bed. The notebook on the table. The forged letters under the mattress.

She tries to whisper her own name.

VERITY
(whispering, almost soundless)
Verity.

The word is rough in her throat. It is a thing her mouth makes that her chest does not quite agree to.

She turns her face to the wall.

A lantern in the alley below her window guts and recovers and holds.

Above, the moon is past full, declining. The almanac has marked tomorrow night as the last night of working hours for the lunar cycle.

She tries, once, to whisper Hale's given name. Casimir. The shape is in her mouth; the air is not. She tries it again. The same.

She presses her gloved hand to her own throat. She has known, since age fourteen, what was promised in the bargain. She had not, until this week, believed it could come early.

Outside, on the harbor, a pilot's bell chimes the third hour. The chime is doubled by the southern point, returning across the water. By the time the second chime fades, Verity has stopped trying to whisper anything at all.

She does not sleep. She lies on her side and counts the lanterns she can see from the window — three at the chandler's wall, one at the cooper's row, one above the salt-works gate. Five lights against the moon. None of them go out before the watch changes.

FADE OUT.

END OF EPISODE 3.
