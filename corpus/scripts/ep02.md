# THE TIDES OF VASSEN
## Episode 2 — "The Cistern"

---

# SCENE 1 — EXT. LOWER WARDS — A FRESHWATER CISTERN — PRE-DAWN

A square stone cistern, fed by a hill spring, set in a small courtyard between two stilted houses. A single iron lantern on a hook above. The lantern is burning. A gust off the harbor, and it goes out.

A WASHERWOMAN, 60s, with a basket of linen on her hip, comes for the morning's water. She lowers her bucket on its rope.

The bucket strikes something soft.

She does not scream. She is a Lower Wards woman; she has hauled drowned men out of cisterns before. She drops the bucket. She walks, fast, for the constable on the corner.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 2 — INT. HALE'S OFFICE — DAWN

Hale at his desk. He has not slept. The locked drawer is open. The folded verses are spread across the blotter. He is checking them line by line, page by page, against the stanza on the slip from his pocket.

VERITY enters without knocking. She has a sealed message in her hand.

VERITY
Lower Wards. The cistern on Cobble Lane.

HALE
A body.

VERITY
A drowned one. Black Watch corporal. His shirt was cut open before he was put in.

Hale stands. He folds the verses. He does not lock them away — he slides them inside his coat lining.

HALE
Carved.

VERITY
The constable on the scene says yes.

HALE
Five lines.

VERITY
He didn't say.

HALE
It will be five.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 3 — EXT. COBBLE LANE — CISTERN — MORNING

The body on the cobbles. Water still draining from the corporal's coat. The shirt has been cut up the centre with a sharp blade — not torn — and folded back like a book.

Five lines. The second stanza of Eline's poem. Hale recognises it before he reads it.

VERITY
Inspector.

HALE
Yes.

VERITY
You knew this would be it.

HALE
I knew the form. Not the man.

VERITY
You know the form how.

A beat.

HALE
The first stanza was on Marsh.

VERITY
The first.

HALE
There are twenty-one.

VERITY
Of how many.

HALE
A poem.

VERITY
Whose.

A long beat. The water in the cistern licks the stone.

HALE
My wife's.

Verity, who has known him seven years, does not move her face. She has been trained out of moving her face.

VERITY
She wrote them.

HALE
In 1897.

VERITY
And the killer has them.

HALE
He has had them five years.

VERITY
How many people have read them.

A pause.

HALE
Two. Including me.

He crouches. The dead corporal's mouth is closed. Hale opens it gently with a gloved thumb. There is no stone.

HALE
No tourmaline.

VERITY
Not always then.

HALE
Not always.

He stands. He looks at the cistern. The water is clear. The body has been there hours.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 4 — INT. DREDGEMASTER'S BARGE — OFFICE — MORNING

The salt-cedar box on the desk is open. Splintered. The bolts that held the lid have been sheared with an iron pry-bar. The Compass is gone.

A single grey feather lies on the desk where the Compass should be.

The Dredgemaster sits in his chair. He has a cup of black tea in his maimed left hand. He has not drunk from it.

Hale enters, alone. Verity has gone to the morgue.

DREDGEMASTER
I told you you'd be back.

HALE
Tell me.

DREDGEMASTER
Past third bell. I was below. Came up to piss off the gunwale. Heard the bolts go. Came in. Empty. Feather.

HALE
The Watch.

DREDGEMASTER
The grey is theirs.

HALE
You're certain.

DREDGEMASTER
Inspector.

He lifts the feather and turns it. The shaft is dyed the same iron-black as the Watch coats.

DREDGEMASTER
And one more thing.

HALE
Yes.

DREDGEMASTER
1879.

A beat. Hale very slowly takes off his hat and sets it on the desk.

DREDGEMASTER
The Surge. The Eastern District.

HALE
I know what 1879 is.

DREDGEMASTER
You don't, Inspector. None of you do. I do.

He stands. He goes to the window. He looks out at the harbor. He does not look at Hale.

DREDGEMASTER
I was a dredgeman. Nineteen years old. We were running the inner channel that night because the Council had ordered a depth check the next morning. We weren't supposed to be there. I saw the inner sluices — the harbor sluices, the great iron ones at the west wall — open. From inside.

HALE
Inside.

DREDGEMASTER
A man on a chain-wheel, Inspector. Three men, in fact. In Council black. I was a hundred yards off, at low light, but I saw chain-wheels turn. Inside means deliberately. Inside means a Council key. Inside means somebody walked into the sluice-house and decided.

HALE
Names.

DREDGEMASTER
Don't have names.

HALE
You said three.

DREDGEMASTER
Three men in black coats. From a hundred yards. In the dark. I'm not a fool, Inspector. I won't perjure on a face I didn't see.

HALE
How many on the Council in '79 are still on the Council now.

DREDGEMASTER
Seven, by my count.

HALE
Names.

DREDGEMASTER
I'll get them to you. Not today. Not in this office. There are ears.

HALE
There always have been.

DREDGEMASTER
There always have. Different ears now.

A beat.

DREDGEMASTER
Inspector.

HALE
Yes.

DREDGEMASTER
Twenty-two years I've taken their salt. I'm not asking absolution.

HALE
I'm not the priest you'd ask.

DREDGEMASTER
No. You're not.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 5 — INT. CATHEDRAL OF THE DROWNED MOTHER — VESTRY — MIDDAY

A small room behind the apse. Stone-cold. A SISTER OF THE ORDER, late 50s, dressed in unbleached wool, sits across a table from Hale. She has a pewter cup of seawater between her hands.

SISTER
Inspector Hale.

HALE
Sister.

SISTER
You wish to ask about the verses.

HALE
You know already.

SISTER
The Order knows what walks in Vassen. We have been here a long time.

HALE
Two copies.

SISTER
Two.

HALE
Mine.

SISTER
And one other.

HALE
Whose.

A pause.

SISTER
The Order will not give you that name without a Council warrant.

HALE
You have the name.

SISTER
The Order has the record. The record is in our confessional ledger. The ledger is sealed by edict.

HALE
The Edict gives you that exemption.

SISTER
And takes from us the right to break it without warrant.

HALE
A Councilor.

SISTER
I have not said.

HALE
You haven't denied.

SISTER
Inspector.

HALE
Sister.

SISTER
We will not refuse you the warrant when you bring it.

HALE
When.

SISTER
When.

A beat. He stands.

HALE
Tell me one thing.

SISTER
If I can.

HALE
Eline gave the second copy herself.

A long pause. The Sister looks at the seawater in her cup.

SISTER
She did not.

HALE
It was taken.

SISTER
That, Inspector, is what the warrant will tell you.

He nods, once. He puts on his hat. He goes.

The Sister does not move for a long minute after he is gone. Then she dips her finger in the seawater and crosses, very small, the line of her own throat.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 6 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STUDY — EVENING

Hale alone. He has Eline's silver wedding ring in his palm. The iron chain is on the desk. He threads the ring onto the chain. He fastens the chain at his throat.

He picks up the almanac. He turns to tonight's date.

ALMANAC INSERT
*15 Drimmar 1903 — Full moon, declining. Cloud cover patchy. Working hours 22:14 to 04:51.*

He puts the almanac in his coat pocket. He does not take it out of that pocket again for the rest of the season.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 7 — INT. CRANE APARTMENT — VERITY'S DOOR — SECOND HOUR OF THE NIGHT

A knock. Not loud. Three flat raps, like Verity's own.

Verity, fully dressed, opens the door a hand's breadth.

A HOODED FIGURE stands on the threshold. The face is in shadow. The figure does not speak. The figure passes a folded paper through the gap.

Verity reads it. She reads it twice.

THE PAPER (insert)
*Sergeant Crane. Your mother will die in agony before the moon sets unless you walk to the southern point of the Bay tonight, at moonrise's third hour, and speak with the shade of Edric Marsh. You will write down what he says. You will leave the writing under the third stone of the seawall at the cottage steps. Any deviation, the patient dies tonight. Tell no one. Bring no one. Burn this.*

She does not burn it. She folds it, slides it inside her coat lining, and looks up. The figure is already gone.

She closes the door. She leans against it. She presses the heel of her hand to her mouth.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 8 — EXT. DREDGEMASTER'S BARGE — ROOFTOP — SAME NIGHT

Mira Vance, on the roof, on her belly, a coil of climber's line at her hip. She had come for the dredge-rope — a coil she has loaned herself, by her own code, three times in two years.

She freezes at the sound of bolts being sheared.

She inches forward to the edge of the roof. Below: a single Black Watch corporal, oilcloth-black, working an iron pry-bar against the salt-cedar box. The bar slips. The bolts give. The corporal lifts the lid.

He removes a small brass instrument. The Compass. Mira does not yet know its name.

The corporal pockets the Compass. He sets a single grey feather on the desk — placed deliberately, not dropped — and walks out.

Mira waits until his footsteps die. Then she follows him along the rooftops.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 9 — EXT. LOWER WARDS — ROOFTOPS / COUNCIL WALL — LATE NIGHT

She is good. She is small, fast, soft-soled. The corporal does not look up.

He turns west at the salt store. He turns north at the chandler's. He passes the Salt Exchange wall, where Mira's drop-spot is, and where she does not stop. He turns east at the cooper's row and approaches the Council Wall.

She loses him at the Wall. There is no rooftop run from the cooper's row to the Council parapets. She perches on the chandler's chimney and watches him pass through a small iron gate that opens, briefly, from inside.

She memorises the gate.

She climbs down.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 10 — EXT. SOUTHERN POINT — BAY EDGE — THIRD HOUR

Verity, hooded, walks the wet sand. She carries a lead pencil and a small oilcloth notebook. She does not carry a lantern.

She passes the seawall steps at the cottage. She does not look at the cottage. She walks fifty yards south, to a place she has been many times before, where a basalt outcrop cuts a small cove from the larger Bay.

The moon is full and direct. The water is black.

She kneels at the waterline. She lays her right palm flat on the wet sand. She lifts her left wrist into the moonlight and turns the silver bracelet up.

She speaks. Her voice is low and steady, in a tongue older than Aelmar — Tide-tongue, taught her by her grandmother on a beach not unlike this one.

VERITY (in Tide-tongue, subtitled)
Edric Marsh. Drowned not, but bled. Carved, but not by me. Speak.

A long beat. The water moves not as water but as something listening.

A VOICE comes up from the Bay. Not loud. The voice of a woman in her early thirties, warm, careful. A voice Verity has not heard before in her life — she does not know it is Eline Hale's.

VOICE FROM THE BAY (Eline Hale's voice, V.O.)
*The clerk who bled at the Eastern wall / wrote what the salt would tell. / He set his name beneath my own / in ink the vault knew well.*

Four lines. Verity writes. The voice goes on.

VOICE FROM THE BAY (cont'd)
*And what the vault has writ in salt, no tide will yet repel.*

A fifth line, no more. The water stills.

Verity finishes writing. She reads the page once. She does not understand what she has written. She does not know whose voice it was.

She stands.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 11 — EXT. SOUTHERN POINT — A LITTLE WAY UP — MOMENTS LATER

She walks back toward the seawall steps. The third stone — third up from the high-water mark — has a small loose chink behind it. She slides the page in. She presses the stone back.

She does not look back at the Bay. She does not weep. She walks home.

A hundred yards inland, the hooded figure waits in the deep shadow of a fishhouse and watches her go past. The figure waits another full minute, then walks down to the seawall and removes the page.

We do not see the figure's face. We see only the gloved hand, the page, and a single ebony walking-stick set against the wall.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 12 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STUDY — JUST BEFORE DAWN

Hale, at his desk. He has not slept. He has Eline's portrait on the desk — a small charcoal, the only one — and he has, for the first time in five years, allowed himself to look at it for longer than a glance.

He says her name aloud.

HALE
Eline.

It is the first time in five years he has said it where another could have heard.

He is alone. No one hears.

The garnet ring at his throat is cold.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 13 — INT. SALT EXCHANGE — RECORDS HALL — MORNING

A vast, quiet hall. Iron-bound ledgers in a wall of shelves. A SALT EXCHANGE CLERK, 30s, prim, gloved, leads Hale down a row.

HALE
I want the contract record for Eline Hale. Copyist work, 1898.

CLERK
Inspector, those are sealed.

HALE
By whom.

CLERK
By the Exchange's own privilege. They're not public records.

HALE
Did she work here.

CLERK
That, I can tell you. Yes. Briefly. Three months early in '98.

HALE
On what.

CLERK
That, I cannot.

HALE
Was anyone else copying the same accounts that quarter.

A beat. The clerk's face does a small thing it does not mean to do.

CLERK
A junior. From the Council records office.

HALE
Marsh.

CLERK
I — the privilege does not allow me—

HALE
Marsh.

A long pause. The clerk swallows.

CLERK
Mr. Marsh, yes.

HALE
Thank you.

He turns. He goes. The clerk stands in the row a long minute, looking at his hands.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 14 — INT. HALE'S OFFICE — LATE MORNING

Verity at the door. She has not slept. Her voice is hoarse, but only a little — it could be a cold, she could be lying about a cold. She is not lying about a cold. She does not know that yet.

VERITY
You wanted me.

HALE
The corporal in the cistern. Name him.

VERITY
Caelis Trent. Twelve years on the Watch. Two complaints in his file — both from me. I arrested him in '98 for striking a salt-runner. He was released on the Council's word.

HALE
A man of theirs.

VERITY
Yes.

HALE
And dead in the morning after the Compass is taken.

VERITY
Yes.

HALE
A pattern.

VERITY
A small one.

HALE
Stay close, Sergeant.

VERITY
(quiet)
Inspector.

She does not say what she might say. She nods, instead, and goes.

He watches the door close. He notices, only after, that her voice was rough at the edges.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 15 — EXT. LIGHTHOUSE POINT — EVENING

Hale on the rail, facing the Bay. The almanac in his coat. The garnet ring against his collarbone.

He does not see, half a mile down the southern beach, the ground where Verity knelt last night. The tide has covered the place where her right palm pressed.

He says her name once more. To no one.

HALE
Eline.

A gust off the harbor takes the sound and gives nothing back.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 16 — INT. CRANE APARTMENT — NIGHT

Verity, alone. The forged letter from her physician lies under her mattress. The page from the Bay-edge writing is gone — left under the third stone of the seawall, retrieved by hands she did not see.

She lies on her side. She does not weep. She listens to her own breath in the dark and hears, in it, a small new roughness she has not heard before.

A lantern in the alley below her window guts down, recovers, and holds.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 17 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT WALL — NIGHT

The Watchman. A new lantern, freshly trimmed. He chalks the patrol-board: *15 Drimmar 1903. All quiet.*

Behind him, in the moonlit silver of the abandoned streets, the water laps an inch higher than it did last night. He does not measure it.

A gull cries.

He does not look at the cistern lane, two blocks down, where the surgeon's cart has come and gone with what is left of Caelis Trent.

He does not look at the Salt Exchange wall, three streets up, where Mira Vance has just chewed and swallowed a slip that named the corporal as a man worth following.

He does not look at the southern point, half a mile out, where a hooded figure has just removed a folded page from behind a third stone.

He does not look at the Lighthouse Cottage, further still, where Inspector Hale has just touched the garnet at his throat and felt that the chain is, at last, the right weight.

He does not look at any of these things because he is a Watchman, and he does not look at things outside his wall.

He hangs the chalk. He waits for the relief.

The lantern, freshly trimmed, holds. The wick is long enough for another two hours, by his guess, which he keeps to himself, because no Watchman has ever been thanked for a wick well-trimmed and no Councilor has ever asked.

FADE OUT.

END OF EPISODE 2.
