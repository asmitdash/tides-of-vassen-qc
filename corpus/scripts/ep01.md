# THE TIDES OF VASSEN
## Episode 1 — "The Eastern District"

Written for screen. Scene headings are H1 markdown so the chunker can split.

# SCENE 1 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT WALL — NIGHT

Rain. Not heavy. Steady, as it has been for nine days.

The wall is twenty feet of cut basalt, kelp-furred at the base. A single iron lantern hangs at the watchpost. The wick gutters once, twice, recovers, then a gust off the harbor takes it for good. Dark.

A BLACK WATCHMAN, oilcloth coat dyed iron-black, lifts a relief lantern from a hook and lights it from a match. The new lantern catches.

Beyond the wall, the abandoned streets of the Eastern District lie in moonlit silver, half-flooded, kelp-furred. Nothing moves on them.

In the distance, on the wet flagstones inside the wall, a smaller light bobs. Someone walking. Someone who should not be there.

The Watchman does not see it. He is checking his own lantern.

# SCENE 2 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — KITCHEN — NIGHT

A spare room. A single oil lamp on a deal table. INSPECTOR CASIMIR HALE, 47, narrow-shouldered, pale grey eyes, sits with his iron flask of salt-water tea and an open almanac of the Lunar Tables, 1903 edition.

He turns a page. He marks tonight's date with a thumbnail crease. The almanac says: *Full moon. Unobstructed.*

A KNOCK at the door. Three flat raps.

HALE
(without looking up)
It's open.

SERGEANT VERITY CRANE, 31, copper-red hair cropped short, sea-green eyes, steps in. Rain in her collar. Constable greys soaked at the shoulders.

VERITY
You'll want your coat.

HALE
Where.

VERITY
Eastern District.

A beat. Hale closes the almanac.

HALE
No one's in the Eastern District.

VERITY
Brenn says one is.

He stands. He takes his oilcloth coat from the peg. He puts on his gloves, leather, even now. He picks up his service revolver from the table and slides it under the coat.

He extinguishes the oil lamp with his thumb and forefinger. The kitchen goes black.

# SCENE 3 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT — A FLOODED STREET — NIGHT

Hale and Verity wade. Water to the ankle, then the shin, then the ankle again. The moon is full and direct. Mnemoncraft weather, by the almanac. Neither of them mentions it.

A CONSTABLE, grey-cloaked, stands beside the body, holding his lantern aloft. He is young. He has not seen one of these before.

CONSTABLE
Inspector.

HALE
Tell me.

CONSTABLE
A clerk. Edric Marsh. From the Council's records office.

HALE
Found by.

CONSTABLE
The Watch. They sent for us inside the hour.

VERITY
The Watch found a body inside their own wall.

CONSTABLE
(quiet)
Yes, ma'am.

Hale crouches. The body lies face up in two inches of standing water. The shirt is open. A verse, five lines, has been cut into the sternum. The cuts are shallow, careful, made with a thin blade. Each letter is a half-inch tall.

He reads it without moving his lips. He does not need to read it twice.

Verity reads it over his shoulder. She frowns.

VERITY
What is that. A poem.

HALE
A stanza.

VERITY
Of what.

He does not answer. He reaches into the dead clerk's mouth with two gloved fingers and removes a black tourmaline memory-stone, cracked lengthwise, grey down the centre. He lays it on his palm.

VERITY
Read.

HALE
Long since.

He turns the stone over. The crack is one inch long. Exactly one inch.

He puts the stone in an evidence pouch and straps the pouch shut.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 4 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT — A FLOODED STREET — CONTINUOUS

Hale stands. He looks down at Marsh's hands. They are clean. The right cuff of his shirt is rolled. Ink on the index finger, faint.

HALE
He was writing today.

VERITY
Council clerks write every day.

HALE
He was writing in the sealed records.

VERITY
How.

HALE
The ink. It's vault ink. Salt-fixed. They use it for nothing else.

Verity looks at him. She is filing this — she has worked with him seven years and she knows when he has placed a card.

VERITY
Inspector. The verse.

HALE
Yes.

VERITY
You've seen it before.

A long beat. Rain in the gutter. The young constable a polite distance away.

HALE
I have.

VERITY
Where.

HALE
(after a moment)
In a drawer.

He walks past her, toward the cart waiting at the wall. He does not say more. She follows.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 5 — INT. HARBOR PRECINCT — EVIDENCE ROOM — NIGHT

A small, low-ceilinged room. Iron bars on the cabinet. A constabulary clerk, MS. FENN, 50s, her ledger open.

Hale sets his coat over a chair. He removes his gloves to handle the cracked stone properly. He takes off his gold wedding band — plain, twenty-one years on his finger — and lays it on the corner of the cabinet to glove up again. It clinks once on the iron.

He turns away to pull a fresh pair of gloves from the supply box.

A HEAVY DOOR opens at the far end of the corridor. A man in Council black walks past, says nothing, does not stop. We do not see his face — only the long fingers, the ebony cane, the sigil ring on the right index finger. The ring catches the lamplight: a compass-rose, gold and black enamel.

Hale, gloving up, hears nothing.

He turns back to the cabinet. He opens the evidence pouch. He places the cracked stone in a tin tray. He logs it in the ledger. Ms. Fenn writes.

He reaches for his ring.

The corner of the cabinet is empty.

He looks at the tray. Not there. He looks at the floor. Not there. He looks at Ms. Fenn.

HALE
Was anyone here.

MS. FENN
A Councilor's man. By privilege of inspection. Three minutes.

HALE
Name.

MS. FENN
The privilege does not require it.

A long beat. Hale's right hand goes to his left ring finger and finds the bare skin. He stands very still.

HALE
(quiet)
Note it in the book.

MS. FENN
Inspector—

HALE
Note it.

She writes. He pulls his gloves on the rest of the way and turns back to the cracked stone.

A lantern on the wall — the duty lantern — guts down a quarter inch in the chimney. No one moves to trim it.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 6 — INT. DREDGEMASTER'S BARGE — OFFICE — DAWN

A salt-cedar desk bolted to the deck. A brass-bolted box on the desk. A salt-white bearded man, OLEN BRASK — the DREDGEMASTER, 63 — stands behind it with his iron-tipped cane in one hand. He has not slept. He is not surprised to see Hale.

DREDGEMASTER
Inspector.

HALE
Brask.

DREDGEMASTER
You'll have heard.

HALE
I have. Tell me what you saw.

DREDGEMASTER
Nothing. Never. Get off my barge.

He says it flat, the way he has said it for twenty-two years.

The box on the desk is open a crack. Inside, on a velvet bed, the Vassen Compass — small, brass, a needle the width of a fingernail. The needle moves. It does not point north. It points at the Dredgemaster's sternum.

The Dredgemaster sees it move. He knows what it means. His face does not change. He puts the heel of his weathered, maimed left hand over the needle and presses down.

DREDGEMASTER
Get.

HALE
Olen.

DREDGEMASTER
Off.

Hale watches him a long second. He notes the hand. He notes the way the Dredgemaster will not lift it. He notes that the salt-cedar box has been open since he walked in, and that the Dredgemaster, who never opens it more than weekly, has just lied to him with the lid up.

He does not call him on it.

HALE
I'll be back.

DREDGEMASTER
Don't.

Hale nods, once, and steps off the barge into the dawn.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 7 — EXT. CRANE FAMILY DOOR — LOWER WARDS — DAWN

A narrow door on a stilted house. A folded paper has been wedged under it. Verity, who has not been to bed, opens her own door and bends.

The seal is her mother's physician's. The handwriting inside is the physician's, copied so faithfully that Verity reads three lines of it without doubt.

PHYSICIAN'S LETTER (V.O., Verity's read)
*Sergeant Crane — your mother has had a poor night. The wasting has advanced. Councilor Thane has, with characteristic generosity, increased his patronage to cover a private nurse. We do not, at present, expect a turn for the worse, but I write to keep you abreast.*

She folds the letter. She holds it a moment. She slides it inside her jerkin, against her ribs, and closes the door.

The lantern in her front hall — burning since last night's late hour — goes out as she crosses the room. The wick has spent itself.

She does not light another.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 8 — INT. HARBOR PRECINCT — HALE'S OFFICE — MORNING

Small office. One window onto the slate-grey harbor. A locked drawer at the bottom of his desk, never opened in office hours.

Hale unlocks it. From a folded oilcloth wrapper he removes a bundle of pages — verses, twenty-one stanzas, in his own careful hand. Eline's poem of 1897, copied out from memory.

He turns to the first page. He reads the first stanza in silence.

The five lines, exactly, are the five lines that were cut into Edric Marsh's sternum.

He sits a long moment with the page on his knee. He does not weep. He has not, in five years, wept where another could see it. The last tear he shed was at his desk, alone, on the third anniversary, and he put it down to the season.

A KNOCK. He folds the page back into the oilcloth, locks the drawer, and only then says—

HALE
Yes.

VERITY (O.S.)
Brenn wants you.

HALE
A moment.

He waits until her footsteps go. Then he opens the drawer once more and takes out a single stanza — not the first — and slides it into his coat pocket. The third.

He locks the drawer.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 9 — INT. HARBOR PRECINCT — COMMISSIONER'S OFFICE — MORNING

COMMISSIONER ALDOUS BRENN, 60, broad, reddened from years of harbor wind, sits behind a desk piled with paper. Hale stands.

BRENN
The Eastern District.

HALE
Yes.

BRENN
A clerk.

HALE
Marsh.

BRENN
A verse.

HALE
A stanza of one.

BRENN
The Council will want a quiet hand on this.

HALE
The Council will get the hand it gets.

A beat. Brenn does not push.

BRENN
A wedding ring is missing from the locker.

HALE
Mine.

BRENN
Yours.

HALE
Yes.

BRENN
We'll find it.

HALE
We won't.

Brenn looks at him. He has known Hale fourteen years. He does not soften.

BRENN
A Councilor's man came through the locker last night. By privilege.

HALE
Which Councilor.

BRENN
The privilege does not require—

HALE
Which.

A beat. Brenn looks at his blotter. He does not answer. He says, instead—

BRENN
Find him, Casimir. The killer. Find him before there's a second.

HALE
There will be a second.

BRENN
You're certain.

HALE
The verse has more than five lines.

He turns and leaves before Brenn can answer.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 10 — EXT. SALT EXCHANGE WALL — A DROP-SPOT — MIDDAY

A narrow alley between the Salt Exchange and a chandler's shop. A loose brick at chest height. MIRA VANCE, 19, small, wiry, brown-skinned, a single thick braid down her back, leans against the wall with a salt-runner's casual ease. She wedges a folded slip into the brick gap.

She does not see Hale come up. He is good at not being seen.

HALE
Mira.

MIRA
Inspector.

She does not pull the slip back. He reads it where it sits.

THE SLIP (insert)
*Marsh in the District. Watch found him but didn't bring him in same hour. Watch knew where to look.*

He nods once.

HALE
Anything else.

MIRA
The Dredgemaster's barge had a light in it past third bell. He's never up past third bell.

HALE
I know. Keep your ears.

MIRA
Always.

HALE
And, Mira—

He hesitates. He does not, in their two years' acquaintance, often offer her anything.

HALE
Stay off the District wall this week.

MIRA
(small grin)
Inspector. I'm always off the wall.

HALE
I mean it.

MIRA
(beat)
I'll think on it.

He walks away.

She removes the slip, balls it, puts it in her mouth, chews it twice, swallows. She has done this since she was twelve. She looks up at the Salt Exchange wall — at a high, salt-rimed window where, on certain afternoons, a man in Council black is sometimes visible.

There is no one there now.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 11 — INT. CONSTABULARY MORGUE — AFTERNOON

A vaulted basement, white-tiled, clean. Marsh on the slab.

The constabulary surgeon, DOCTOR LEVEN, 50s, lifts the sheet to the chin. Hale, Verity, and the surgeon are alone.

LEVEN
Death by exsanguination. The cuts to the chest were antemortem. Made on a moonlit night, by my guess — the bleeding pattern's wrong for indoor work.

HALE
Why.

LEVEN
The hand was steady the way a hand is steady when it knows the moon is on it.

VERITY
That's superstition.

LEVEN
Sergeant. Forgive me. Thirty-one years on this slab.

VERITY
Forgiven.

LEVEN
The stone in the mouth was cracked before it was placed. I would say a week before. The crack's edges are dulled — a fresh crack would be sharp.

HALE
A week.

LEVEN
Roughly.

HALE
Read by whom.

LEVEN
That, Inspector, is what you're paid for.

A beat. Hale lifts Marsh's right hand. The ink on the index finger is dry but unmistakable. Salt-fixed black, the kind made only at the Salt Exchange's own ink-house.

HALE
He copied something today.

VERITY
What.

HALE
That, Sergeant, is what you're paid for.

A small, thin smile from Verity — the closest thing to warmth he has shown her this week.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 12 — INT. CRANE FAMILY APARTMENT — LATE AFTERNOON

A small room. A single window onto the harbor. Verity sits on the edge of a thin bed. The forged letter is in her lap. Beside her, on the table, a tin cup of tea has gone cold.

She does not weep. She reads the letter again. She slides it under the mattress.

She lifts her left wrist and turns the silver bracelet absently — a habit, an old one. The bracelet has worn a faint groove at her wrist over seventeen years.

A new sound. Outside: a gull, then a second gull. She looks toward the window.

Above her: the moon, climbing. Not yet full overhead. Two hours yet.

She does not move.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 13 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — STUDY — NIGHT

Hale, alone. The folded copy of the verses is on the desk. The cracked memory-stone, in its evidence tin, is beside it. He has not opened the tin.

He lifts Eline's silver wedding ring with the garnet from a small cedar chest on the desk. It hangs from his fingers a moment. He has not held it in five years.

His own gold ring is gone. The bare line of skin on his left hand catches the lamp.

He sets Eline's ring back in the chest. He closes the lid.

He does not look in the mirror.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 14 — EXT. LIGHTHOUSE POINT — NIGHT

The decommissioned lighthouse. The point juts into the Bay. Hale stands at the rail above the rocks, almanac in one hand, the page held open in the moonlight.

He has come up here to confirm what the almanac already tells him. The moon is full. The cloud cover is thin. Mnemoncraft weather. A reading night.

If anyone in Vassen is reading, they are reading tonight.

He looks down at the rocks. He looks at the line of dark water. He says her name in his head and not aloud.

He is about to turn back when, far down the southern beach — half a mile, maybe — a single figure walks the tideline.

He cannot tell, at this distance, who. He holds the almanac up to break the moon-glare. The figure crouches at the water's edge. The figure stays.

Hale watches a long time. The figure does not light a lantern. The figure does not look up.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 15 — EXT. BAY EDGE — SOUTHERN BEACH — NIGHT — CONTINUOUS

Closer. We do not yet know it is Verity. We see only: a hooded coat, a pale wrist, a silver bracelet glinting once and then covered by a sleeve. A folded slip of paper held in gloved fingers.

She kneels at the water's edge. She does not speak. She listens.

The sea makes a small sound, not a wave, not a word.

She rises. She walks back the way she came.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 16 — EXT. EASTERN DISTRICT WALL — LATE NIGHT

The Watchman from Scene 1, on his second relief lantern. Behind him, in the moonlit silver of the abandoned streets, water laps a flagstone an inch higher than the night before.

He marks his patrol-board with chalk: *14 Drimmar 1903. All quiet.*

He hangs the chalk on its hook.

A gull cries.

A beat.

CUT TO:

# SCENE 17 — INT. LIGHTHOUSE COTTAGE — KITCHEN — VERY LATE

Hale at the deal table. The almanac closed. The cracked memory-stone in its tin, unopened. The folded stanza in his pocket.

He lays his hand flat on the wood. He considers the missing band.

HALE
(to himself, very quiet)
Twenty-one years.

He extinguishes the oil lamp with his thumb and forefinger. The kitchen goes black.

The bare line of skin where his gold band has been for twenty-one years is the only thing that catches the moon through the window.

FADE OUT.

END OF EPISODE 1.
