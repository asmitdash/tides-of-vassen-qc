# The Tides of Vassen — Show Bible

**Season 1 · 5 Episodes · Set in the year 1903**

A noir-fantasy detective drama. One season. Five episodes. The story is closed-ended; this is not a backdoor pilot.

---

## World

Vassen is a port city on the eastern coast of the kingdom of Aelmar, founded in the year 1612 on a delta of black silt and basalt. For two and a half centuries it was the kingdom's wealthiest harbor — salt, amber, and the strange white kelp called *moonweed* that grows only off Vassen's reefs. Then, on the night of the 14th of Drimmar, 1879, a tide rose forty-one feet in under three hours and did not recede for nine days. When the waters fell, the Eastern District — eleven streets, three guildhalls, two thousand four hundred and sixteen souls — was gone. The official record names this the **Tide Surge**. The Council of Nine declared the Eastern District abandoned by edict the following spring. It has not been entered legally since.

The city has been sinking at a steady, surveyed rate of one inch per year ever since. By 1903, the lower steps of the Salt Exchange are wet at high tide. The Council insists this is unconnected to the Surge.

Vassen in 1903 is a place of guttering oil lamps, iron-shod boots on wet flagstones, and a coastline that is six feet closer to every door than it was when the current generation was born. The wealthy live in the Upper City on the basalt shelf. The poor live in the Lower Wards, where the streets flood twice a month at the spring tides and the children are taught to swim before they are taught to read.

## Magic System — Mnemoncraft

Memory-magic, called **mnemoncraft** in formal speech and **stone-reading** in the Lower Wards, is the only practiced magic in Aelmar. Its rules are hard, narrow, and well-known to every citizen of Vassen. They are never bent for plot convenience.

**Hard rule 1 — Moonlight requirement.** Mnemoncraft functions only under direct, unobstructed moonlight. A cloud across the moon ends a working mid-sentence. A roof ends it absolutely. New-moon nights, eclipses, and the hours between dawn and dusk are dead time. Practitioners plan their workings by an almanac called the *Lunar Tables*, published annually by the Royal Observatory.

**Hard rule 2 — One-shot consumption.** A memory may be extracted from a living mind into a polished stone of black tourmaline (a **memory-stone**). The stone holds the memory perfectly — every sense, every thought-edge — until it is read. The act of reading destroys both the memory and the stone: the tourmaline cracks lengthwise and goes grey. There is no copying. There is no second reading. A memory, once stored and once read, is gone from the world.

**Hard rule 3 — Iron disruption.** Iron within six feet of an active working scrambles it. Constables of Vassen carry iron cuffs for this reason; a cuffed prisoner cannot be read or have memories taken. The Council Chamber is roofed in iron sheeting.

**Hard rule 4 — The Tide Witch exception.** Tide Witches — women born with a matrilineal gift — can speak with the drowned dead of Vassen Bay. The gift requires only seawater and moonlight; it does not require a stone. The cost is the witch's voice: every Tide Witch loses the use of her speaking voice by her fortieth year and must thereafter communicate by writing or signed gesture. There has never been more than one Tide Witch alive at a time.

**Hard rule 5 — The Lantern Edict, 1881.** Two years after the Tide Surge, the Council of Nine outlawed all mnemoncraft within Vassen's walls. Possession of a memory-stone, charged or uncharged, is a hanging offense. Practitioners flee inland or work in cellars, hoping the moon finds them through a skylight. The Edict is enforced unevenly; the wealthy buy exemptions through the Salt Exchange.

## Geography

**The Upper City** sits on the basalt shelf, fifty feet above mean tide. The Council Chamber, the Salt Exchange, and the Cathedral of the Drowned Mother stand here. The streets are paved in cut basalt and named after the nine founding houses.

**The Lower Wards** spread between the shelf and the harbor. Fishermen, salt-workers, dockmen, smugglers. The streets flood; the houses are built on stilts driven into the silt.

**The Eastern District** — abandoned since 1879. Walled off by Council order. Patrolled by the Black Watch, who shoot trespassers without challenge. The buildings still stand, kelp-furred and salt-rimed, half-submerged at high tide. No one is permitted to enter except by personal warrant of a Councilor.

**Lighthouse Cottage** stands on the southern point. The lighthouse itself was decommissioned in 1882; the cottage beside it is the residence of Inspector Casimir Hale.

**Vassen Bay** — black water, tidal, full of the drowned of the Surge. The shipping channel runs north-south between the harbor and the open sea.

## Major Factions

**The Constabulary of Vassen.** Two hundred and forty officers under Commissioner Aldous Brenn. Inspector Hale leads the Special Cases office: four officers, one ledger-clerk. They handle anything the regular precincts cannot or will not.

**The Council of Nine.** Hereditary. Their sigil rings stamp every law, treaty, and judicial sentence in the city. The current Councilor for the Eastern Ward is **Thelonius Thane**, who has held the seat since 1887 and who has, by his own count, never set foot inside the ward he represents.

**The Black Watch.** A private militia in Council pay, eighty strong, who guard the Eastern District wall. They wear oilcloth coats dyed with iron-black and answer only to the Council.

**The Drowned Mother's Order.** A quiet, dwindling clergy who tend the Cathedral and conduct the sea-burials. They keep one of the only legal memory-stones in the city, used to record dying confessions — a privilege granted by exemption in 1881 and renewed every five years.

**The Salt Exchange.** Not a faction so much as an institution: a guild-bank that sets the salt-to-crown ratio and holds, in its sealed vaults, ledgers older than the Council itself.

## Themes

- **What we keep by forgetting.** Mnemoncraft destroys what it preserves. The city that pretends the Surge was an act of God, not negligence. The widower who has stopped saying his wife's name aloud.
- **Tide as time.** The water rises one inch a year. Nothing stops it. The case will not be solved before the next high tide.
- **The price of women's voices.** Tide Witches lose their speech. Eline Hale's verses are in a hand that can no longer write. Verity Crane stops speaking by the close of episode four.
- **Sealed records.** Every faction keeps a ledger no one else may read.

## Tone & Voice

Terse. Procedural in its bones. The dialogue is short and Northern-cold; the prose breath is longer when the magic surfaces, and only then. No one in Vassen speaks in exclamations. No one explains the magic system aloud — it is the air the characters breathe, and they would no more define it than a Londoner would define rain.

Median sentence length nine words. Scenes end one line before the audience expects. The camera lingers on objects: a cracked stone, a wedding ring on a chain, an ink-blotted ledger page. Music is sparse — solo cello, sea-organ, occasional unaccompanied women's voices.

## Recurring Motifs

- **Lanterns going out.** Every episode opens on a lantern being extinguished — by wind, by hand, by the reach of a wave. The final shot of the season is a single lantern relit.
- **The wedding ring on a chain.** Hale wears Eline's ring on a chain around his neck after his own is stolen in episode one. The chain is iron.
- **The cracked stone.** The first cracked memory-stone of the season is found in episode one's victim's mouth. The last is the one Hale chooses not to read in episode five.
- **The ledger.** Three ledgers braid the season: the Black Ledger (Thane's bribes), the Salt Exchange's sealed accounts, and the Drowned Mother's confessional record.
- **Verses carved in skin.** The killer's signature. Each verse is a stanza from a poem Eline Hale wrote in 1897 and never published; only Hale and one other person ever read it.
- **One inch.** Mentioned at least once an episode. The rate the city sinks. The width of the Compass needle. The crack in a memory-stone.

## What This Season Is Not

This is not a show about magic. It is a show about a man who has stopped grieving and is forced to start again. The magic exists because in this city, in this season, grief is the murder weapon.
